# Privacy, Restriction, and Erasure

## Three-tier promise

HAP promises only:

1. **Permanent opaque commitment:** Bitcoin can prove that a committed package existed.
2. **Revocable within compliant infrastructure:** indexes, locators, hosted evidence, decryption/opening material, and serving can be restricted or erased.
3. **Not reversible outside compliant infrastructure:** screenshots, mirrors, downloads, social reposts, and hostile archives cannot be recalled.

Crypto-shredding is containment, not universal deletion and not reputational repair.

## On-chain minimisation

Bitcoin commitments contain no human-readable allegation, name, media, locator, or directly guessable personal-data hash. Personal and evidentiary material remains off-chain.

## Subject and restriction records

The append-only graph supports:

- `subject_response`
- `person_impact_notice`
- `restriction_notice`
- `withdrawal_notice`
- `legal_adjudication`

These objects are declarations or legal context, not automatic global invalidation.

## Identity issuers

No universal identity provider exists. A credential issuer may attest that a responder is related to a subject, while each client decides whether it recognises that issuer.

The protocol verifies the issuer's signature. It does not make the credential's underlying claim omniscient or grant greater response prominence.

## Jurisdiction

Operators apply their own disclosed legal and jurisdictional policies. A restriction honoured in one jurisdiction may be ignored elsewhere.

Portable restriction records can encourage voluntary recognition, but a permissionless network cannot guarantee worldwide enforcement without introducing a global control authority.

## Privacy limitations

Pseudonymity is available but anonymity is not guaranteed. Network metadata, Bitcoin funding, reused signing keys, writing style, media metadata, and external behaviour can reveal identity.
