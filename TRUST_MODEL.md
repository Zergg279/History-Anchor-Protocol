# HAP Trust Model

## Four epistemic types

### Protocol fact

Reproducible from exact bytes, signatures, HAP links, and Bitcoin state.

Examples: a signature verifies; two evidence files have the same SHA-256; a record targets another record; a batch commitment is confirmed in Bitcoin's active chain.

### Signed declaration

HAP proves who made the declaration and that it was not altered. It does not prove the declaration is honest.

Examples: a publisher declares a source; a device signs a capture statement; an organisation declares custody.

### Derived inference

A signed analytical overlay names its inputs, method/ruleset, version, output, and optional confidence. It can be reproduced, challenged, or ignored.

### External-world claim

A statement about physical reality. HAP preserves evidence for and against it but never makes it consensus truth.

## Source independence

Independence is not established by different keys, accounts, URLs, organisations, or timestamps. Reposts and derivatives may share one upstream source.

V1 deterministically identifies exact byte reuse. Stronger source-family conclusions remain analytical overlays. Analyst counts and confirmation counts are descriptive, never votes.

## Analyst identity

Anyone may publish a provenance assertion. No permissioned analyst class or universal reputation score exists.

Clients may expose key continuity, disclosed affiliation, methodology, reproducibility, corrections, conflicts, and recognised credentials. Any weighting belongs to a disclosed view policy and cannot alter protocol validity.

## Dominant-client risk

A popular client may shape perception through defaults even when the protocol permits alternatives. This is an ecosystem centralisation risk, not something cryptography can eliminate.

Reference clients therefore expose raw proofs, context, profile version, local trust choices, and a content-derived view manifest. They must not call a private ranking "the protocol's verdict."
